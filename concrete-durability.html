Concrete fails in Winter. A tales as old as time?

Many people ask the same question each year, often with a hint of frustration. They pour a new driveway, a patio, or a backyard slab, everything looks perfect at first. And then after a winter or two, the new concrete just deteriorates. What went wrong? They then ask around, and will likely hear a familiar answer. "Probably no air in the concrete. Give it some air next time so the water can freeze safely." It sounds simple and in many ways it is right. As a person who studies concrete, and spent almost of my life in the tropical climate of Vietnam, where winter is not even part of the conversation, I may also provide the same answer. Even before truly experiencing cold weather, I had leaned about the importance of air in concrete. It is something really basic, something really universal, passed down from engineer to another, from the old generation to the next generation. But that is also what makes me pause for a minute and think. If the explanation is there, if even someone like me, who has only lived through a few winters, can repeat the same answer as experts in cold regions, then is the problem really that simple? Of course no, we keep repeating and telling the same thing because we just understand part of it, not all of it!

My research focuses on understanding the role of ionic transport, surface resistivity (SR), and rapid chloride permeability (RCP) in controlling salt scaling behavior.

Key Topics

  • Freeze-thaw mechanisms (hydraulic pressure, cryogenic suction)
  • Salt scaling behavior under 3% NaCl
  • Transport properties (SR, RCP)
  • Performance-based durability classification
